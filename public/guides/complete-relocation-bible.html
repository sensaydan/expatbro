Part 1: Before You Leave

Chapter 1: The Decision ‚Äî Is This Right For You?

Let me be straight with you: Moving to Latin America isn't for everyone. The Instagram posts showing beach sunsets and $5 cocktails don't show the bureaucracy, the cultural adjustment, or the moments when you just want something familiar.

I've been doing this for six years. 110 countries. I've met hundreds of people who tried the expat life ‚Äî some thrived, some crashed and burned within months. The difference? Knowing what you're getting into.

You'll Probably Love It If:

  • You're adaptable. Plans change. Things don't work the way you expect. The ability to roll with it is everything.
  • You're self-sufficient. You can't rely on customer service culture. You solve your own problems.
  • You're curious about other cultures. Not just tolerating them ‚Äî genuinely interested.
  • You have location-independent income. Or savings. The job market for foreigners is limited.
  • You're running toward something, not just away. Escaping a bad situation is fine, but having a positive vision helps.

You'll Probably Struggle If:

  • You expect first-world efficiency. Things move slower. Accept it or suffer.
  • You're not willing to learn any Spanish/Portuguese. English gets you by in tourist areas, but that's not really living there.
  • You need predictability. Latin America runs on its own clock. Literally.
  • You're highly risk-averse. There's more chaos, more uncertainty. That's part of the adventure.
  • You can't handle being the outsider. You will always be a foreigner, even after years.
üéØ The Real Test Before committing, do a 1-month trip. Not in a resort ‚Äî get an Airbnb in a normal neighborhood, take public transport, shop at local markets. If you love it after a month of actual living (not vacationing), you're ready.

The Mindset Shift

In North America and Europe, systems are designed to be efficient. Things work. Customer service exists. When something breaks, you complain and it gets fixed.

In Latin America, you need what I call "gringo zen." Things will go wrong. Your internet will cut out during an important call. Your landlord will vanish for a week. The government office will require a document they didn't mention.

You have two choices: rage against the inefficiency (and burn out), or develop patience and problem-solving skills that will serve you for life.

I've watched high-powered executives crumble because they couldn't handle a 30-minute wait at the bank. I've watched chill backpackers thrive because they found the absurdity entertaining rather than infuriating.

Your "Why" Matters

Why do you want to do this? Be honest.

  • Lower cost of living? Valid. You'll live better for less. But the savings aren't as dramatic as YouTube gurus claim.
  • Adventure and travel? Valid. Latin America is a launchpad to explore an entire hemisphere.
  • Dating opportunities? Let's be real ‚Äî it's a factor for many. We'll cover this honestly later.
  • Escaping winter? Valid. Permanent sunshine changes your mental health.
  • Political/social escape? Valid. Different problems, different freedoms.
  • Career break or remote work? Valid. Best time to try new things.

There's no wrong answer, but knowing your motivation helps you choose the right destination and set realistic expectations.

Chapter 2: Pre-Departure Checklist (30 Items)

This is the checklist I wish I had before my first move. Do these before you leave ‚Äî some are annoying, but future you will thank present you.

Documents & Identity (1-3 months before)

  • Passport validity check: Must be valid for 6+ months after your planned return (most countries require this)
  • Make passport copies: Physical copies and digital (store in cloud + encrypted USB)
  • Apostille important documents: Birth certificate, degree, marriage/divorce if applicable ‚Äî some countries require apostilles for residency applications
  • International driver's license: Get from AAA ($20) ‚Äî not required everywhere but useful
  • Photo copies: Get 10+ passport photos in the standard size (you'll need them for various bureaucracy)

Finances (1-2 months before)

  • Get a no-foreign-fee debit card: Charles Schwab or Wise are best ‚Äî reimburse ATM fees worldwide
  • Get a no-foreign-fee credit card: Chase Sapphire, Capital One Venture, or similar ‚Äî for purchase protection
  • Set up Wise (TransferWise): Essential for moving money between currencies cheaply
  • Notify your banks: Tell them you're traveling or they'll freeze your cards
  • Reduce recurring expenses: Cancel subscriptions you won't use, pause gym memberships
  • Set up online banking: Make sure you can do everything digitally
  • Check your credit report: Fix any issues before you leave (harder to dispute abroad)

Health (1-2 months before)

  • Doctor visit: General checkup, stock up on prescriptions (3-6 month supply)
  • Dentist visit: Get everything done ‚Äî dental work abroad is cheap but not always reliable
  • Eye exam: Update prescriptions, get backup glasses/contacts
  • Vaccinations: Check CDC recommendations for your destination (Hepatitis A/B, Typhoid, Yellow Fever for some areas)
  • Travel insurance: Get a policy that covers medical evacuation ‚Äî SafetyWing or World Nomads are popular for long-term travelers
  • Prescription letters: Get a doctor's letter for any medications you're carrying (especially controlled substances)

Technology & Communication (2-4 weeks before)

  • Unlock your phone: Call your carrier ‚Äî you need an unlocked phone for local SIMs
  • Download offline maps: Google Maps and Maps.me for all destinations
  • Get a VPN: ExpressVPN or NordVPN ‚Äî essential for accessing home content and security on public WiFi
  • Set up Google Voice/Skype number: Keep a US/UK number for bank verifications, etc.
  • Password manager: Get 1Password or Bitwarden if you don't have one
  • Back up everything: Laptop to cloud, phone to cloud ‚Äî assume your devices could get stolen

Home Base (2-4 weeks before)

  • Mail forwarding: Use a virtual mailbox service (US Global Mail, Anytime Mailbox)
  • Bills on autopay: Any recurring payments should be automatic
  • Storage or sell: Decide what to do with your stuff ‚Äî storage costs add up, selling is liberating
  • Give someone power of attorney: A trusted person who can handle emergencies
  • File taxes strategy: Talk to an accountant about Foreign Earned Income Exclusion (US) or departure taxes (varies by country)
  • Cancel or pause unused services: Cable, utilities, subscriptions
‚úÖ Pro Tip Create a "digital go bag" folder: passport scan, insurance info, emergency contacts, important passwords, bank numbers, embassy addresses. Store it encrypted in the cloud. You'll access it more than you expect.

  • Be paranoid on public WiFi: Always use VPN. Or just use your phone hotspot.

Chapter 5: 10 Mistakes That Will Cost You

I've watched hundreds of people attempt the expat life. These are the mistakes I see over and over. Avoid them.

1. Not Learning Any Spanish/Portuguese

You don't need to be fluent. But "I'll just use Google Translate" is a recipe for isolation and getting ripped off. Basic conversational ability changes everything ‚Äî people treat you differently, doors open, you feel less like a tourist.

Fix: Get to A2/B1 level before you go, or intensively once you arrive. 2-3 months of daily practice is enough for basics.

2. Signing a Long-Term Lease Too Fast

That neighborhood seemed great when you visited for a week. After a month, you realize it's far from everything, noisy at night, or just doesn't vibe with you. Now you're locked in for a year.

Fix: Airbnb for the first month. Walk the neighborhood at different times. Then sign a 3-month lease max. Then go longer.

3. Flashing Money and Electronics

Yes, you have an iPhone. No, you don't need to use it on the street in sketchy areas. Petty crime is opportunistic ‚Äî don't make yourself a target.

Fix: Blend in. No jewelry, use an old phone outside, split your cash into multiple spots.

4. Only Hanging Out With Other Expats

The expat bubble is comfortable. But you didn't move to Colombia to hang out with other Americans complaining about local inefficiency. You'll learn nothing, improve your Spanish never, and miss the point.

Fix: Force yourself into local situations. Take salsa classes at a local studio, not an expat-oriented one. Join a local gym. Date locals.

5. Not Having Health Insurance

"Healthcare is cheap here, I'll just pay out of pocket." Then you break your leg, need surgery, and it's $15,000. Or you get seriously ill and need medical evacuation ‚Äî that's $50,000+.

Fix: SafetyWing or World Nomads for travel insurance. Local insurance if you're settling long-term. This is not optional.

6. Underestimating Bureaucracy

That visa extension? It'll take 3x longer than the official time. That bank account? You'll visit the branch 4 times. Accept this reality.

Fix: Build in buffer time for everything official. Start processes early. Have your documents ready in triplicate. Be patient but persistent.

7. Burning Through Savings

"It's so cheap here!" is how people justify lifestyle inflation. Yes, it's cheaper than NYC. That doesn't mean you should eat out every meal, take Ubers everywhere, and party every weekend.

Fix: Set a monthly budget BEFORE you arrive. Track expenses. Live like you plan to stay, not like you're on vacation.

8. Ignoring Local Laws and Customs

You're a guest in someone else's country. Their rules apply, even if they seem dumb to you. This is especially important around drugs ‚Äî Latin American prisons are not fun.

Fix: Research local laws. Be respectful of customs. When in doubt, follow local lead.

9. No Backup Plan

Your card gets blocked, your laptop dies, your Airbnb cancels last minute. What do you do?

Fix: Always have backup: second card from different bank, backup phone, emergency cash, friend/family who can help remotely.

10. Expecting Home

It's different here. That's the point. If you wanted everything to work like home, you should have stayed home. Complaining that "things are so disorganized" or "people are always late" will make you miserable and everyone else annoyed.

Fix: Embrace the difference. Find what you love about it. The chaos is part of the adventure.

‚úÖ The Meta-Lesson Most mistakes come from treating this like an extended vacation instead of a life transition. The mindset shift from "tourist" to "resident" is the most important thing you'll do.

The Reality on the Ground (2025-2026)

Venezuela is not the failed state many assume. In major cities, restaurants are open, nightlife exists, and the upper-middle class lives normally. But the contrasts are stark. Much of the population struggles with hyperinflation and shortages.

For travelers: you'll experience a country frozen in time, with 1960s American cars, colonial architecture, and prices that make Colombia look expensive. A $20 dinner would feed you for a week here.

How Money Works

The official exchange rate vs. the black market rate creates opportunity (and complexity). You'll use USD cash and exchange locally. Credit cards often don't work. Crypto is increasingly accepted.

Chapter 16: Safety ‚Äî Street Smarts That Work

Let's be real: Latin America has higher crime than where you're coming from. But it's also not the warzone your parents imagine. Understanding the risks ‚Äî and how to mitigate them ‚Äî lets you enjoy life without paranoia.

The Reality Check

  • Petty crime (pickpocketing, phone snatching): Common, preventable with awareness
  • Armed robbery: Happens in certain areas/times. Hand over your stuff. It's not worth your life.
  • Express kidnapping: Rare for foreigners but exists. Victim is driven to ATMs to withdraw cash.
  • Serious violent crime: Usually targets locals, often drug-related. Random violence against foreigners is rare.

Universal Safety Rules

  1. Don't look like a target. No flashy jewelry, watches, or electronics on display. Dress down, not up.
  2. Be aware of your surroundings. Headphones out in sketchy areas. Look around, not at your phone.
  3. Trust your instincts. If something feels wrong, it probably is. Leave.
  4. Know your route. Look up directions before you leave, not on the street corner.
  5. Avoid ATMs at night. Use bank ATMs inside during daylight.
  6. Keep minimal cash on you. Enough for the day, split between pockets.
  7. Have a dummy wallet. Old wallet with expired cards and small bills. Hand this over if robbed.
  8. Uber > random taxis. Always. You want a digital trail and driver accountability.
  9. Don't fight back. Your stuff is replaceable. You are not.

Night Safety

  • Uber/Cabify/Didi ‚Äî never hail taxis at night
  • Stay in well-lit, populated areas
  • Don't walk home drunk alone
  • Let someone know where you're going
  • Share your live location with a friend when going to new places

Dating Safety

  • First dates in public places only
  • Tell a friend where you're going and who with
  • Don't accept drinks from strangers (or leave drinks unattended)
  • Trust your gut about people
  • In Colombia especially: be aware of scopolamine ‚Äî see earlier warning

Home Safety

  • Choose buildings with 24-hour portero (doorman)
  • Don't open the door for unexpected visitors
  • Use a safe for passport and backup cash
  • Lock everything ‚Äî don't get complacent
  • Get to know your neighbors ‚Äî they'll look out for you
‚úÖ Perspective Millions of people live normal, safe lives in these cities every day. The goal isn't paranoia ‚Äî it's awareness. Take reasonable precautions, live your life, and you'll be fine.

Chapter 20: Taxes & Legal ‚Äî Don't Get Screwed

I'm not a lawyer or accountant. This is general guidance ‚Äî get professional advice for your specific situation.

The US Expat Situation

Americans are taxed on worldwide income regardless of where they live. Yes, even abroad. Key things:

  • FEIE (Foreign Earned Income Exclusion): Exclude up to ~$120,000 of foreign earned income from US taxes. Must be out of US for 330 days in a 12-month period OR establish tax residency abroad.
  • FBAR: If you have $10,000+ in foreign bank accounts (combined), you must file FBAR by April 15.
  • FATCA: Foreign accounts over $50,000-200,000 (depending on status) require Form 8938.
  • State taxes: Some states (California, NY) are aggressive about claiming you still owe taxes. Establish residency elsewhere or properly exit.
‚ö†Ô∏è Get Help Expat taxes are complex. Mistakes can cost thousands. Budget $500-1,500/year for an expat-specialized accountant. Worth every penny.

Other Nationalities

Most countries don't tax worldwide income if you're not resident. But rules vary:

  • UK: Non-resident after a year abroad usually means no UK tax on foreign income
  • Canada: Residency-based, but departure rules are strict
  • Australia: Similar to UK ‚Äî residency determines taxation
  • EU: Varies by country. Some (Spain, Italy) have special regimes for new residents.

Tax Residency Abroad

Spending time in a country can make you tax resident there:

  • Mexico: 183+ days = potential tax residency
  • Colombia: 183+ days = potential tax residency
  • Brazil: Complex rules ‚Äî get advice if staying long-term

Tax residency can mean filing obligations, but often rates are lower than home countries.

Structuring for the Long Term

Common strategies (consult professionals):

  • Establish LLC or company in low-tax jurisdiction
  • Use tax treaties to reduce withholding
  • Split time between countries strategically
  • Consider residence in a territorial tax country (Panama, Paraguay, Costa Rica)

Legal Stuff

  • Keep copies of all visa stamps and immigration paperwork
  • Don't work illegally ‚Äî consequences can include deportation and bans
  • Understand local laws ‚Äî drug laws are often stricter than you think
  • Have a basic will and power of attorney
  • Know how to contact your embassy
üìÅ Record Keeping Keep copies of: all visa stamps, entry/exit records, apartment leases, utility bills, bank statements. Some of these prove foreign residence for tax purposes. Digital backup of everything important.

Chapter 22: When Things Go Wrong

Bad things happen. Here's how to handle them.

Lost/Stolen Passport

  1. File police report (denuncia) ‚Äî required for replacement
  2. Contact your embassy ASAP
  3. Bring: police report, passport photos, identification you have, copy of old passport if available
  4. Emergency passport usually issued in 24-48 hours
  5. May need to rebook travel (embassy can help with airlines)

Prevention: Keep passport locked in safe. Carry a color copy for daily ID.

Medical Emergency

  1. Call emergency services (911 in Mexico, 123 in Colombia, 192 in Brazil)
  2. For non-emergency but urgent: Uber to private hospital ER
  3. Contact your insurance provider immediately
  4. Call family/emergency contact
  5. Keep insurance cards and emergency contacts in phone

Getting Robbed

  1. Don't resist. Your stuff isn't worth your life.
  2. Hand over what they ask for
  3. Get to safety, then process
  4. File police report (for insurance, though recovery unlikely)
  5. Cancel cards immediately (use your backup phone)
  6. Contact embassy if passport taken

Natural Disaster/Political Crisis

  1. Register with your embassy (STEP program for Americans)
  2. Follow local news in Spanish (Google Translate the headlines)
  3. Have emergency kit: water, food, flashlight, battery pack, cash
  4. Know evacuation routes
  5. Have exit plan (flights, land routes)

Running Out of Money

  • Have emergency fund accessible (not tied up in investments)
  • Budget for 3+ months buffer before you go
  • Wise or PayPal for emergency transfers from family
  • Western Union works almost everywhere
  • Embassy can help with repatriation as last resort

Visa Issues

  • Never overstay ‚Äî consequences include fines and bans
  • Build buffer time for extensions (bureaucracy is slow)
  • Consult an immigration lawyer if complicated situation
  • Embassy can provide guidance but can't fix everything
‚úÖ Preparation Is Prevention Most crises are survivable with preparation: copies of documents in cloud, emergency cash hidden at home, backup cards, insurance that covers evacuation, local emergency contact who speaks your language. Set these up before you need them.

The Marriage Path

Marriage to a local accelerates everything:

  • Immediate temporary residency in most countries
  • Faster path to permanent residency (often 1-2 years)
  • Faster path to citizenship (often halved)

Don't get married for a visa. But if you're in a real relationship, know it helps.

The Investment Path

Most countries have investor visas:

  • Mexico: Invest in Mexican business or property
  • Colombia: Invest ~$170,000 in business or property
  • Brazil: Invest ~$100,000 in productive activity

Property alone rarely qualifies ‚Äî must be productive investment or company creation. Consult immigration lawyer for current thresholds.
